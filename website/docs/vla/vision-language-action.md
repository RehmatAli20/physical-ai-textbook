# Vision–Language–Action (VLA)

Vision–Language–Action (VLA) systems represent a major shift in how robots interact
with the world. Traditional robots follow predefined instructions, while VLA-enabled
robots can understand what they see, interpret human language, and take appropriate
actions.

In a VLA system, perception, reasoning, and motion are tightly connected. The robot
does not simply react; it understands intent and context before acting.

---

## What Does Vision Mean in Robotics?

Vision refers to how a robot perceives its environment using sensors such as cameras
and depth sensors.

Through vision, a robot can:
- Detect objects
- Identify obstacles
- Recognize people
- Estimate distances and positions

**Example:**  
A robot equipped with a camera can detect a chair in front of it and determine whether
the chair blocks its path.

---

## What Does Language Mean?

Language allows humans to communicate with robots using natural words instead of
technical commands.

Language understanding enables robots to:
- Interpret instructions
- Understand intent
- Respond meaningfully

**Example:**  
The instruction “Bring me the book from the table” contains multiple pieces of
information:
- Object: book
- Location: table
- Action: bring

---

## What Does Action Mean?

Action refers to physical movement and interaction with the environment.

Actions include:
- Walking
- Turning
- Grasping objects
- Avoiding obstacles

A VLA system connects vision and language to determine **which actions** should be
performed and **in what order**.

---

## Why Combine Vision, Language, and Action?

Separating these components limits robot intelligence. Combining them allows robots
to behave more naturally and intelligently.

**Example Scenario:**  
A human says:  
> “Go to the door and open it.”

The robot must:
1. Use **vision** to locate the door
2. Use **language** to understand the command
3. Use **action** to move and manipulate the door

This integrated behavior is the core idea behind VLA systems.
